The Hidden Complexity of RAG

Most tutorials show you the tip of the iceberg. Here's what's below the waterline — and why it's where 90% of production failures hide.
Krishil Agrawal · 18 min read · ~3,600 words · Beginner to Advanced
Above water RAG for Beginners — the 7 concepts every tutorial covers. Fast to understand, fast to break in production.
Below water RAG for Builders — the 15+ concepts tutorials skip. This is where production systems live or die.

There is a version of RAG that takes two hours to build. Embed your documents. Store them in Chroma. Embed the query. Retrieve top-5 chunks. Pass to the LLM. Ship it. You will get a demo that impresses everyone in the room. You will get a prototype that "works."

Then you put it in production. Users ask slightly different questions than you expected. The retrieval returns irrelevant chunks. The LLM hallucinates because it got noisy context. Sensitive customer data leaks into wrong responses. Latency spikes because you are embedding every query cold. Nobody told you any of this was coming, because every tutorial stopped at the demo.

This post is the complete iceberg map — above the waterline and below it. You will understand not just what these concepts are, but why they exist and what failure they prevent.

TL;DR — the 4 things to know first
  • The beginner RAG stack (LangChain, embeddings, vector DB, prompt templates) is real and necessary. It is not wrong — it is just incomplete for production.
  • Poorly evaluated RAG systems hallucinate in up to 40% of responses even when the correct source document was retrieved. The problem is not always retrieval — it is generation quality on noisy context.
  • 70% of RAG systems in production still lack systematic evaluation frameworks, making it impossible to detect quality regressions. Evaluation is not optional infrastructure.
  • The builder's layer — reranking, query reformulation, PII masking, hallucination detection, latency/accuracy tradeoffs, retrieval caching — adds significant engineering effort but prevents the silent failures that break production systems.

Above the Waterline:
RAG for Beginners

These are the 7 concepts every RAG tutorial covers. They are the foundation. Without them, nothing else works. The mistake is not learning them — the mistake is thinking they are enough.

01 · Framework
LangChain / LlamaIndex Basics
The orchestration layer that stitches retrieval to generation. LangChain gives you composable chains; LlamaIndex specializes in document indexing and querying. Both abstract the boilerplate so you focus on logic.
02 · Architecture
Basic Retrieval Pipeline
The core loop: ingest → chunk → embed → index → retrieve → generate. Understanding this sequence is the prerequisite for understanding every optimization that comes later.
03 · Ingestion
Data Loaders (PDFs, CSVs, Docs)
Before you can retrieve, you need clean text. PDF extraction fails on scanned images, tables, and multi-column layouts. Data loading is where hidden quality problems begin — long before the LLM is involved.
04 · Representation
Chunking and Embeddings
Text is split into chunks (fixed-size, semantic, or structural), then each chunk is converted to a dense vector by an embedding model. Chunk size directly determines retrieval quality — too small loses context, too large drowns the LLM.
05 · Storage
Vector Databases
Pinecone, FAISS, Chroma, Qdrant, Weaviate — stores that support approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search over high-dimensional vectors. ANN trades 100% recall for millisecond query times across millions of vectors.
06 · Interface
Prompt Templates
The structural format that places retrieved context and the user question into the LLM's input. A poorly designed prompt template causes the LLM to ignore retrieved context and answer from training knowledge — defeating the entire purpose of RAG.
07 · Generation
LLMs + Retrieval
The generative model that reads the context and produces an answer. The model choice matters less than you think — a well-designed retrieval pipeline with an average model outperforms a top model with poor retrieval.
Basic RAG pipeline — the beginner's version
💡
The demo problem: This pipeline works well on the documents you indexed and the queries you tested. It breaks on edge cases, domain-specific vocabulary, PII-containing documents, ambiguous queries, and any user behavior you did not anticipate. The above is not wrong — it is just incomplete without everything below the waterline.

Below the Waterline:
RAG for Builders

This is where production systems diverge from demos. Each concept below exists because something broke — in someone's production system — before they added it. These are not optional improvements. They are the lessons of shipping RAG at scale.

Tier 1 — Pre-Retrieval: Before You Even Search

Pre-processing
Preprocessing & Cleaning
Raw document extraction is messy. PDFs produce garbled text, merged columns, repeated headers, and OCR errors. Tables extract as random character sequences. HTML produces tag noise. Preprocessing normalizes encoding, removes boilerplate, fixes extraction artifacts, and standardizes whitespace — before any chunk ever reaches the embedding model.
PII Masking
PII Masking
Production documents contain names, emails, phone numbers, SSNs, credit card numbers, and addresses. If these are chunked and indexed, they will be retrieved and surfaced in responses to users who should never see them. PII detection (using spaCy NER, Microsoft Presidio, or a custom NER model) and masking must run before indexing — not after.
Query Layer
Query Reformulation
Users ask vague, ambiguous, or single-word questions that vector search handles poorly. "Tell me about the contract" is not a useful embedding. Query expansion rewrites it to "service level agreement payment terms termination clauses penalties." Techniques include HyDE (generate a hypothetical document, embed that), query expansion via LLM, and multi-query retrieval (run 3-5 reformulations and merge results).
# Query reformulation with HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embeddings
from anthropic import Anthropic

client = Anthropic()

def hyde_retrieve(user_query: str, vectorstore) -> list:
    """Generate a hypothetical answer, embed it, use that for retrieval."""
    hypothetical_response = client.messages.create(
        model="claude-sonnet-4-6", max_tokens=256,
        messages=[{
            "role": "user",
            "content": f"""Write a 2-paragraph document that would ideally answer
this question. Be specific and use domain vocabulary.
Question: {user_query}"""
        }]
    ).content[0].text

    # The hypothetical doc uses domain vocabulary the real docs also use
    # Its embedding lands closer to relevant chunks than the sparse query
    results = vectorstore.similarity_search(hypothetical_response, k=5)
    return results

# Example: query "what happens if I miss a payment"
# HyDE generates: "If a customer fails to make a scheduled payment, the
# contract specifies a grace period of 5 business days after which..."
# That document embeds near the actual contract terms — better retrieval
💡
Why query reformulation matters: User queries are written in user language. Your documents are written in domain language. The embedding space bridges semantic similarity, but users asking "how do I cancel?" will not reliably match documents that say "termination clause" or "service discontinuation policy" without reformulation.

Tier 2 — Post-Retrieval: After You Find Candidates

Post-Retrieval
Reranking (Cross-Encoders)
Your bi-encoder embedding model retrieves the top-50 candidates fast but imprecisely. A cross-encoder reranker then scores each candidate against the query jointly — slower but far more accurate. Cross-encoders like ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2 process (query, doc) pairs together, capturing fine-grained relevance that embedding similarity misses. Reranking is the single highest-ROI improvement for most RAG systems.
Evaluation
Evaluation Metrics & Retrieval Testing
Context Precision (are retrieved chunks relevant?), Context Recall (did you retrieve all relevant chunks?), Faithfulness (are claims in the answer supported by context?), Answer Relevancy (does the answer address the question?). 70% of RAG systems in production lack systematic evaluation — making it impossible to know when a code change breaks retrieval quality.
Operations
Latency vs Accuracy Tradeoff
Every layer you add — reranking, query expansion, multi-query retrieval, LLM-as-judge evaluation — adds latency. The LiveRAG 2025 challenge showed reranking improved MAP by 52% but increased query time from 1.74s to 84s. Production systems need an explicit latency budget and architectural decisions that respect it — not every accuracy improvement is deployable.
# Cross-encoder reranking — the accuracy boost that costs latency
from sentence_transformers import CrossEncoder

# Stage 1: Bi-encoder retrieves candidates fast (millions of docs, <50ms)
candidates = vectorstore.similarity_search(query, k=50)

# Stage 2: Cross-encoder re-scores top-50 candidates slowly but accurately
reranker = CrossEncoder("cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2")

pairs = [[query, doc.page_content] for doc in candidates]
scores = reranker.predict(pairs)          # (query, doc) processed together

# Sort by reranker score, take top-5 for LLM context
ranked = sorted(zip(scores, candidates), reverse=True)
top_docs = [doc for _, doc in ranked[:5]]

# Why this matters:
# Bi-encoder: "cat" and "dog" have similar embeddings → both retrieved
# Cross-encoder: sees full (query + doc) pair → scores by actual relevance
# Cost: 50 cross-encoder inference calls vs 1 ANN search
# Benefit: context precision improves significantly on ambiguous queries

Tier 3 — Generation: Quality, Safety, and Control

Safety
Hallucination Detection & Control
Even with correct documents retrieved, LLMs hallucinate in up to 40% of responses on noisy context. Faithfulness evaluation (RAGAS) decomposes each answer into atomic claims and checks each against retrieved context. A faithfulness score below 0.8 means 20% of claims have no retrieval support — those are hallucinations. Regulated industries should target 0.9+.
Reliability
Error Analysis & Feedback Loops
Production failures must become test cases. Every hallucination, every mis-retrieved chunk, every user-reported error should be captured, labeled, and added to the evaluation dataset. Without this loop, the same failures recur. With it, your system continuously improves from real-world usage patterns — not just from your synthetic test set.
Architecture
Custom Retriever Architectures
Hybrid (BM25 + dense), multi-hop (retrieve → reason → retrieve again), self-RAG (the model decides when to retrieve), GraphRAG (knowledge graphs for entity reasoning). Different architectures solve different failure modes. Choosing the right one requires knowing which failure mode is actually hurting your system — which requires the evaluation infrastructure from Tier 2.
# Faithfulness evaluation with RAGAS
from ragas.metrics import faithfulness, context_precision, answer_relevancy
from ragas import evaluate
from datasets import Dataset

# Your RAG pipeline output
data = {
    "question": ["What is the penalty for late payment?"],
    "answer":   ["There is a 5% late fee after 30 days."],
    "contexts": [[
        "Section 4.2: Late payment fees of 5% apply after 30 calendar days.",
        "Section 4.3: Disputed invoices are exempt from late fees."
    ]],
    "ground_truth": ["A 5% late fee applies after 30 calendar days."]
}

result = evaluate(Dataset.from_dict(data),
                  metrics=[faithfulness, context_precision, answer_relevancy])

print(result)
# faithfulness: 1.0      → every claim is grounded in retrieved context
# context_precision: 0.8  → 80% of retrieved chunks were actually needed
# answer_relevancy: 0.95  → answer directly addresses the question

# Action thresholds (production):
# faithfulness < 0.8  → investigate hallucination sources
# context_precision < 0.5 → retrieval is surfacing too much noise
# answer_relevancy < 0.7  → generation is going off-topic

The Deepest Layer — Rarely in Tutorials

Efficiency
Retrieval Cache Management
Embedding the same query twice is wasteful. Semantic caching stores query embeddings and their results, returning cached answers for semantically similar future queries. This cuts latency from 500ms to under 10ms for cache hits and reduces embedding API costs. Tools: GPTCache, Redis with vector similarity, or a simple FAISS index of recent queries.
Scale
Knowledge Distillation for Smaller Models
GPT-4-class retrieval at GPT-3.5 cost. Distillation trains a smaller embedding or generation model on the outputs of a larger model — preserving most of the accuracy at a fraction of the inference cost. Critical when you need sub-100ms latency at thousands of queries per second.
Infrastructure
Hardware Constraints
Embedding models need GPU. Rerankers need GPU. The LLM needs GPU. Each query can touch all three in sequence. Hardware budgeting — which models need GPU, which can run CPU-only, when to cache, when to batch — directly determines whether your RAG system is economically viable at scale.
Continuous Learning
Continuous Fine-Tuning Based on User Feedback
Collect thumbs-up/thumbs-down signal. Label the retrievals that led to good vs bad answers. Use that signal to fine-tune the embedding model or the reranker on your domain. Systems that do this improve steadily; systems that do not plateau at demo-level quality.
Security
Secure Retrieval
Row-level security for vector databases. If user A should not see user B's documents, you cannot just filter post-retrieval — the ANN search itself must be scoped. Metadata filters, tenant isolation, and access control at the vector store level are prerequisites for any multi-tenant RAG deployment.
Reasoning
Multi-Hop Retrieval
Some questions require chaining multiple retrievals. "Who signed the contract with the highest penalty clause?" requires finding the contract, finding the penalty clauses, comparing them, then looking up the signatory. Multi-hop RAG agents retrieve → reason → reformulate → retrieve again — often 3-5 hops deep before generating the final answer.
Responsibility
Ethical Bias Checks
Embedding models encode historical biases. Retrieval can systematically surface or suppress documents along demographic lines. Evaluation should include retrieval bias audits — do queries about different user groups return systematically different quality results? For regulated industries, this is a compliance requirement, not a nice-to-have.

The 5 Evaluation Metrics That Define Production Quality

Every metric below is measurable, automatable, and tells you a different thing about where your system is failing. You need all five — they are complementary, not redundant.

Metric What it measures Target What low score means
Faithfulness Are answer claims supported by retrieved context? ≥ 0.80 (≥0.90 regulated) LLM is hallucinating from training memory
Context Precision What fraction of retrieved chunks were actually needed? ≥ 0.75 Retrieval returning noisy irrelevant chunks → confuses LLM
Context Recall Did retrieval find all relevant information? ≥ 0.80 Important information exists in corpus but was not retrieved
Answer Relevancy Does the answer directly address the question? ≥ 0.75 Retrieval pulled adjacent-but-wrong chunks; generation is tangential
Hallucination Rate % of responses with claims not in retrieved context ≤ 5% for most apps 5% = 1 in 20 responses contains fabricated information

The teams I see building reliable production RAG are the ones who set up RAGAS evaluations before they set up Pinecone. They treat evaluation as the foundation, not as the polish at the end. Three Things to Take Away

First: the beginner stack is not wrong — it is incomplete. LangChain, embeddings, vector databases, and prompt templates are the necessary foundation. Every builder-layer concept depends on having that foundation in place. The mistake is treating the foundation as the finished building.

Second: evaluation is the gateway to the builder layer. You cannot fix what you cannot measure. Context precision, context recall, faithfulness, and answer relevancy tell you specifically which part of your pipeline is failing — retrieval, reranking, or generation. Without these metrics, improvements are guesswork. With them, every engineering decision has a target. 70% of RAG systems in production lack these — which is why 70% of production RAG systems are stuck at demo quality.

Third: the deepest layers are where competitive moats are built. Continuous fine-tuning on user feedback, semantic caching, multi-hop retrieval, secure tenant isolation — these are not features you add once. They are systems you build and maintain. The teams shipping reliable enterprise RAG at scale have invested engineering time in every layer of the iceberg, not just the visible tip.

The iceberg image that inspired this post captures it perfectly: what you see above water is a small fraction of what keeps the whole structure stable. The stability is entirely in what you cannot see.
